The Intelligence Age: Human Wisdom in the AI Era

Chapter 16: AI in Creativity and Media

16.1 The AI Creativity Spectrum

In August 2022, Jason Allen submitted an artwork to the Colorado State Fair’s fine arts competition. Théâtre D’opéra Spatial was a luminous, dreamlike scene in a rococo hall. It won first place. Then the backlash came. Allen had created the piece using Midjourney. He had typed prompts, iterated, selected, and refined — but he had not painted a brushstroke.

“This is the definition of cheating,” one artist wrote.

Allen responded: “I’m not apologizing. I won. I followed the rules.”

The fair’s rules said nothing about AI. After the controversy, they added a new category: “AI-assisted digital art.” But the question Allen’s win raised — what is creativity when a machine can generate art? — would never be settled by a rule change.

This question sits at the heart of the AI creativity spectrum. The spectrum ranges from passive tool to autonomous creator — and understanding where a given capability falls is the foundation of strategic creative practice.

Allen’s win at the fair was not cheating. It was an early sign of a spectrum that would reshape every creative industry — from tool to generator, from human-only to human-plus-AI. The question was never “can AI create?” It could. The question was “what do we value?”

16.2 The Industry Disruption

In 2023, Hollywood stopped. The Writers Guild of America struck for 148 days. SAG-AFTRA struck for 118 days. At the center of both strikes: AI.

The WGA’s demand was specific: AI could not write or rewrite literary material. AI-generated material could not be considered “source material.” A writer could choose to use AI with the company’s consent, but could not be required to do so. SAG-AFTRA went further: digital replicas — AI-generated performances using an actor’s likeness — required explicit consent and fair compensation.

The studios eventually agreed. But the strikes revealed an industry bracing for impact.

The strikes did not ban AI. They established a precedent: AI can assist, but it cannot replace the creative worker without consent and compensation. The WGA contract’s AI provisions became a template for publishing, music, and even software development.

16.3 The Copyright Earthquake

In December 2023, the New York Times sued OpenAI and Microsoft. The claim: millions of NYT articles were used to train ChatGPT without permission. OpenAI argued that training on public web data was fair use — the same way a human learns from reading. By 2026, the case was still unresolved. Its outcome would determine the legality of every large language model ever trained.

“The copyright question,” one legal scholar said, “is not about the past. It is about who owns the creative economy of the future.”

The four-question copyright framework:

QuestionCurrent Status (2026)Key PrecedentProjected Resolution
Is training on copyrighted data fair use?Unresolved — NYT v. OpenAI pending; Getty v. Stability AI (UK): infringementGetty UK ruling: license requiredHybrid: opt-out + collective licensing (2027-2028)
Can AI-generated works be copyrighted?No — US Copyright Office: human authorship requiredThaler v. Copyright Office (2024): upheldNarrow exception for “creative input” threshold
Who owns AI-assisted works?Case-by-case — depends on human contribution levelCopyright Office guidance: “sufficient creative control”Likely: significant human authorship → copyrightable
Are AI outputs derivative works?Untested in courtTraining data as “learning” vs. “copying” debateUnclear; depends on fair use resolution

The photography analogy from 1884 — Burrow-Giles v. Sarony — haunts the debate. The Supreme Court ruled that a photograph could be copyrighted because of “posing, arrangement, lighting” — human creative choices. The parallel question for AI: does prompt engineering constitute sufficient creative input? The current consensus: simple prompts (<10 words) do not; complex iterative prompt sequences with specific creative intent may. The boundary is being tested in case law as of 2026.

16.4 The Authenticity Crisis

In 2025, Medium removed 40% of its English-language articles in a single purge. Most were AI-generated SEO spam. LinkedIn was flooded with AI-generated “thought leadership” — posts that looked like human insight but were generated from prompts. Facebook and Instagram were deluged with AI-generated images — inspirational quotes over fake landscapes, fake travel photos, fake religious art.

The platform moderators could not keep up. The volume was too high.

“The problem is not that AI content is bad,” a platform researcher said. “The problem is that there is too much content that is good enough to be trusted but not trustworthy.”

The 2026 consensus: reliable detection of AI-generated text at the single-document level is not possible. The detection-generation arms race has no terminal endpoint. The only sustainable solution is provenance infrastructure — tracking content from creation, rather than detecting AI after the fact.

16.5 The Artists Who Adapted

In 2022, Refik Anadol was already a decade ahead of the AI art debate. His installations — Machine Hallucinations, Winds of Yawanawa — used AI to transform vast datasets into immersive visual experiences. His work was not about “AI making art.” It was about an artist using AI to see patterns no human could perceive.

Anadol understood something that the panic crowd missed. AI was not the end of creativity. It was a new instrument — like the camera, like the synthesizer, like Photoshop. The instrument did not replace the artist. It expanded what the artist could do.

Holly Herndon, a composer, took a different approach. She trained an AI on her own voice, creating Holly+ — an AI vocal model anyone could use to generate songs in her voice. She established a licensing structure: share revenue if you make money; free for personal projects. She gave others her instrument. Her value was in the curation and context — the human judgment about what to share.

The photographer’s advantage was particularly telling. Photographers initially feared AI image generation. Then they discovered that AI could not replicate their greatest asset: being in the room. A war photographer, a wedding photographer — their value was not in the technique of making the image. It was in being present, in choosing the frame, in capturing a moment that no prompt could describe.

16.6 The Music Industry Reckoning

In April 2023, an anonymous creator posted a song to TikTok — a duet between AI-generated voices of Drake and The Weeknd. “Heart on My Sleeve” went viral, amassing 15 million plays before platforms took it down. Universal Music Group demanded takedowns. The song was submitted for Grammy consideration — and rejected.

But the question lingered: if the song was good, why did it matter who performed it?

By 2025, Spotify was adding an estimated 120,000 AI-generated tracks per day. The platform had 100 million total tracks — most of them AI-generated. Streaming royalties, already thin, were being divided into ever-smaller slices. The music industry faced a new crisis.

In 2026, the industry reached a tentative consensus: AI training required explicit consent from rights holders. Streaming platforms would label AI-generated tracks. Revenue from AI-generated music would be split between the rights holders of the training data and the platform. The agreement was imperfect, widely contested, and better than nothing.

16.7 The Provenance Economy

By 2026, the most valuable creative brands were not AI companies. They were human-centered platforms — Substack (readers pay for specific writers), Patreon (fans fund specific artists), and are.na (a curated visual collection). These platforms grew exactly because AI content devalued generic creation. When anyone could generate anything, the only thing worth paying for was someone’s specific voice.

The authenticity paradox of 2026: when provenance is known, listeners prefer human musicians, readers pay more for human authors, and audiences seek out human curators. The market did not collapse. It bifurcated. On one side: a vast ocean of AI-generated commodity content. On the other: a growing archipelago of human creators whose value comes from the one thing AI cannot replicate — a real person with real experience, real taste, and the courage to be vulnerable.

16.8 Video Games: The Creative Synthesis

The video game industry, larger than movies and music combined at $200B+ annually, faced the most complex AI integration challenge. Game development was notorious for “crunch” — mandatory overtime as artists struggled to create thousands of assets. AI tools promised to eliminate the bottleneck.

Roblox announced in 2024 that its AI game creation tools had increased asset generation speed by 10×, allowing any player to become a creator. The company’s stock rose 30% on the announcement. But the real story was not speed — it was the ethical frontier that opened.

ElevenLabs partnered with game studios in 2025 to generate voiced dialogue for every NPC — a task previously impossible at scale. For the first time, RPGs could have fully voiced worlds. But voice actors argued they should be paid for every generated line derived from their voice model. The Screen Actors Guild negotiated a specific gaming contract in 2026: AI-generated voices required actor consent and per-project compensation.

The most successful AI use in games was invisible. AI that helped developers create faster — but left storytelling, world-building, and player experience design to humans. Players did not want to talk to an AI game master. They wanted to talk to a human designer who had built a world with intention.

16.9 The New Creative Literacy

The most important shift was not technological but cultural. A new creative literacy was emerging.

The old literacy asked: “Can you write, draw, compose?” The new literacy asks: “Can you direct AI with intention, edit its output with taste, and infuse the result with your unique perspective?”

The most successful creatives in 2026 were not pure technicians. They were curators and directors — people who understood the medium well enough to guide AI, judge its output, and add the human element that machines could not replicate.

The new creative literacy framework:

Can you master the medium?Can you judge AI output with taste?Curation portfolio quality
Can you produce original work?Can you infuse AI output with perspective?Signature recognizability
Can you meet deadlines?Can you manage AI iteration speed?Production velocity vs. quality

The final irony: AI solved the production problem but worsened the discovery problem. The curators — book reviewers, influencers, librarians — became more important, not less. The instrument does not make the musician. The brush does not make the painter. And the AI does not make the artist. Creativity has always been about what humans bring — their experience, their pain, their joy, their unique way of seeing. AI can generate. Only humans can create.
