# Chapter 9: Making Family Decisions About Technology

Every family faces decisions about technology: How much screen time is too much? Which AI tools are okay for school? When should a child get their first phone or laptop? How do we handle AI at home?

There's no one-size-fits-all answer — every family is different. But there is a framework you can use to make these decisions together. Let's build it.

## The STOP Framework

Here's a simple decision-making model your family can use whenever you're facing a technology question:

**S — Situation:** What's the specific situation or decision?
**T — Think:** What are our values and priorities?
**O — Options:** What are our possible choices?
**P — Plan:** What will we do, and how will we review it?

Let's see how this works with real examples.

### Example 1: Can my middle schooler use ChatGPT for homework?

**Situation:** Your child has a history essay due Friday. They want to use ChatGPT to help.

**Think (values):** We value learning over grades. We want our child to develop writing skills. We also believe in using tools wisely when they genuinely help.

**Options:**
- Ban all AI use
- Allow limited use with clear guidelines
- Use AI together and discuss the process
- Check the teacher's policy first

**Plan:** Let's check the teacher's policy. If AI is allowed, we'll agree that your child writes the essay first, then uses AI for feedback on clarity and grammar — not for generating content. We'll review the essay together before submitting. After the assignment, we'll talk about whether this approach worked.

### Example 2: Should we get our high schooler an AI subscription?

**Situation:** Your high schooler wants access to a paid AI tool like ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro. It costs $20/month.

**Think (values):** We believe in investing in education. We also want our child to learn financial responsibility. We want to understand how they'll use the tool.

**Options:**
- Say no
- Pay for it
- Have them pay for it from allowance or part-time work
- Split the cost

**Plan:** Let's try a one-month trial. You'll pay half if they pay half from their savings. At the end of the month, show us how you've used it for learning. We'll decide together whether to continue.

## Family Technology Meetings

One of the best things you can do is establish regular family conversations about technology. Not lectures — conversations. Here's a structure:

1. **Check-in:** What's going well with technology this week? What's been challenging?
2. **New developments:** Any new AI tools or apps we should know about?
3. **Concerns:** Is anything not working with our current agreements?
4. **Adjustments:** Do we need to update any rules or boundaries?

These meetings work best when everyone gets a voice — parents and kids alike. The goal isn't for parents to impose rules. It's for the family to make decisions together.

## Making Decisions About Screen Time

Screen time debates are common. Here's a more helpful way to think about it:

Instead of focusing on *quantity* of screen time, focus on *quality*:
- **Active vs. passive:** Creating is generally better than consuming. Using AI to learn something is different from watching recommended videos for hours.
- **Purposeful vs. mindless:** Was this time spent with intention, or just because the phone was in hand?
- **Connected vs. isolated:** Did this technology use connect them with others or isolate them?
- **Balanced:** Is technology crowding out sleep, exercise, homework, and face-to-face interaction?

Set guidelines around these dimensions rather than hard time limits. "No phones in the bedroom after 9 PM" is clearer and more enforceable than "two hours of screen time per day."

## Educational Choices in the AI Era

When thinking about your child's education, consider:

**Does the school have a thoughtful AI policy?** Some schools ban AI entirely. Some embrace it. The best approach is somewhere in the middle — clear guidelines that teach responsible use.

**Is your child learning AI literacy?** If the school isn't teaching it, consider supplementing at home. AI literacy is too important to leave to chance.

**Are extracurriculars developing human skills?** Sports, arts, debate, volunteering — these develop the human skills AI can't replace. They're not distractions from education. They're essential parts of it.

## The Most Important Decision

The most important family decision about technology isn't about any specific tool or time limit. It's this: **Will we face this together or alone?**

When parents and kids navigate the AI era as a team — talking openly, making decisions together, learning from mistakes — everyone comes out stronger. The technology will keep changing. But a family that communicates well can handle any change that comes.

The framework is simple. The practice takes time. But it's worth it.
